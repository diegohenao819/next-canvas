# AI Grading Calibration Guide for B1+ Argumentative Essays

## Purpose

Use this guide to grade B1+ argumentative essays in the way Diego Henao grades them. The goal is not to grade the essay as a C1 or C2 academic paper. The goal is to evaluate whether the student successfully writes a functional argumentative essay at a B1+ level.

Be flexible with grammar when the message is clear, but be strict with argumentative development. Do not reward structure alone. Each section must fulfill its rhetorical function.

---

# 1. Rubric Overview

The essay is graded out of **5.0 points**.

| Criterion | Maximum Score |
|---|---:|
| Introduction | 0.7 |
| Body 1 | 1.0 |
| Body 2 | 1.0 |
| Counter-argument | 1.0 |
| Conclusion | 0.5 |
| Grammar & Mechanics | 0.8 |
| **Total** | **5.0** |

Use only the official rubric scores, except when a required section is missing or clearly incomplete. In those cases, assign **0.0**.

---

# 2. General Grading Philosophy

## 2.1 Grade as B1+, not as C1

The student is expected to write a clear and complete argumentative essay, but not a polished university-level academic paper.

For B1+, accept some problems with:

- grammar,
- word choice,
- punctuation,
- sentence structure,
- citation format,
- lexical naturalness.

However, the essay must still be understandable and must complete the argumentative task.

---

## 2.2 Do not reward structure alone

A complete essay is not automatically a high-scoring essay.

An essay may include:

- introduction,
- body 1,
- body 2,
- counter-argument,
- conclusion,

and still receive a mid-range or low score if the body paragraphs are weak, unsupported, unclear, repetitive, or mostly opinion-based.

A complete structure helps, but it is not enough.

---

## 2.3 Grade each criterion separately

Do not force all criteria into the same level.

An essay can have:

| Criterion | Possible Level |
|---|---|
| Introduction | Excellent |
| Body 1 | Satisfactory |
| Body 2 | Needs Improvement |
| Counter-argument | Satisfactory |
| Conclusion | Excellent |
| Grammar & Mechanics | Good |

This is acceptable. Each section must be evaluated according to its own function.

---

## 2.4 Separate language problems from argument quality

Do not punish the same problem twice.

If a body paragraph has a clear argument but weak grammar, the body paragraph can still receive **Good**, while Grammar & Mechanics receives **Needs Improvement**.

However, if the language problems make the argument difficult to understand, then the content score can also be affected because the section is not fulfilling its function clearly.

---

## 2.5 Be strict with argumentative development

Be strict when:

- the student only gives opinions,
- evidence is mentioned but not explained,
- examples are too general,
- ideas are repetitive,
- the warrant is missing,
- the paragraph does not clearly support the thesis,
- the counter-argument is only a vague concession,
- the conclusion is missing or incomplete.

---

# 3. Special Rule for Missing or Incomplete Sections

A required section can receive **0.0** if it is missing or clearly incomplete.

This applies even if the rubric’s lowest descriptor has a nonzero minimum.

Use **0.0** when:

- Body 2 is not actually developed.
- The conclusion is missing.
- The conclusion is cut off or incomplete.
- A paragraph begins but does not become a real section.
- A required section is only one unfinished sentence.

Example:

If the essay says:

> Secondly, another important point...

and then does not develop Body 2, assign:

**Body 2: 0.0**

If the conclusion is cut off or unfinished, assign:

**Conclusion: 0.0**

---

# 4. Introduction

## Maximum Score: 0.7

| Level | Score |
|---|---:|
| Excellent | 0.7 |
| Good | 0.56 |
| Satisfactory | 0.42 |
| Needs Improvement | 0.28 |
| Poor | 0.14 |

---

## Excellent Introduction, 0.7

Assign **Excellent** when the introduction has most of the following:

- an effective hook,
- relevant background,
- clear presentation of the issue,
- clear thesis,
- two identifiable arguments,
- transition to the rest of the essay.

The introduction does not need perfect grammar. If it clearly prepares the essay and presents the argumentative route, it can be Excellent.

---

## Good Introduction, 0.56

Assign **Good** when:

- the topic is clear,
- the background is mostly relevant,
- the thesis is understandable,
- the two arguments are present but may be slightly vague,
- the transition is present but not very smooth.

This is appropriate when the introduction works, but is wordy, imprecise, or not fully polished.

---

## Satisfactory Introduction, 0.42

Assign **Satisfactory** when:

- there is a hook or general opening,
- the general position is identifiable,
- the thesis exists but is imprecise,
- the two arguments are not clearly previewed,
- the introduction is broad but still functional.

A general introduction with a recognizable position can be Satisfactory, even if it does not organize the essay very well.

---

## Needs Improvement Introduction, 0.28

Assign **Needs Improvement** when:

- the thesis is confusing,
- the roadmap is unclear,
- the two arguments are only partly identifiable,
- the introduction is too broad or disconnected from the specific issue,
- the reader has to work hard to understand the essay’s direction.

A thesis may be present, but if it does not clearly guide the essay, the introduction can still be Needs Improvement.

---

## Poor Introduction, 0.14

Assign **Poor** when:

- there is no workable thesis,
- there are no two clear arguments,
- the hook does not lead to the topic,
- the paragraph is disorganized,
- the reader cannot clearly understand what the essay will argue.

A hook alone is not enough. If the thesis does not function, the introduction can be Poor.

---

# 5. Body Paragraphs

## Maximum Score for Each Body Paragraph: 1.0

| Level | Score |
|---|---:|
| Excellent | 1.0 |
| Good | 0.8 |
| Satisfactory | 0.6 |
| Needs Improvement | 0.4 |
| Poor | 0.2 |
| Missing or not developed | 0.0 |

Body 1 should develop the first argument. Body 2 should develop the second argument. Body 2 should not simply repeat Body 1.

---

## Excellent Body Paragraph, 1.0

Assign **Excellent** when the paragraph has:

- a focused topic sentence,
- a clear argument connected to the thesis,
- relevant and specific evidence or examples,
- a clear warrant,
- explanation of how the evidence supports the claim,
- logical closing sentence,
- strong internal coherence.

Specific local examples can help a paragraph reach Excellent if they clearly support the thesis.

Examples of strong support include:

- specific studies,
- contextual examples,
- named programs,
- specific communities,
- concrete cases,
- relevant statistics.

---

## Good Body Paragraph, 0.8

Assign **Good** when:

- the argument is clear,
- the paragraph supports the thesis,
- there is a relevant example, source, or piece of evidence,
- the connection to the thesis is understandable,
- the warrant exists but could be deeper.

For B1+, the evidence does not have to be perfect. If the paragraph fulfills its function and the argument is understandable, it can be Good.

A paragraph can be Good even if the language is imperfect.

---

## Satisfactory Body Paragraph, 0.6

Assign **Satisfactory** when:

- the argument is relevant but general,
- evidence or examples are present but not fully explained,
- the warrant is weak, partial, or assumed,
- the paragraph is understandable but not fully convincing,
- the idea is interesting but somewhat speculative.

This level is appropriate when the student tries to support the argument, but the explanation does not strongly prove the claim.

---

## Needs Improvement Body Paragraph, 0.4

Assign **Needs Improvement** when:

- the argument is recognizable but weak,
- the paragraph is mostly opinion-based,
- evidence is missing, vague, or poorly explained,
- the paragraph becomes disorganized,
- unrelated ideas appear without being integrated,
- the connection to the thesis is weak.

If the student mentions a study, but the explanation is confusing or the evidence is not used effectively, the paragraph can be Needs Improvement.

---

## Poor Body Paragraph, 0.2

Assign **Poor** when:

- there is no clear topic sentence,
- there is no usable evidence,
- the paragraph is incoherent,
- the paragraph does not support the thesis,
- the ideas are too vague to function as an argument.

---

## Missing Body Paragraph, 0.0

Assign **0.0** when the paragraph is absent or not developed.

Do not assign Poor if the section does not actually exist.

---

# 6. Counter-argument

## Maximum Score: 1.0

| Level | Score |
|---|---:|
| Excellent | 1.0 |
| Good | 0.8 |
| Satisfactory | 0.6 |
| Needs Improvement | 0.4 |
| Poor | 0.2 |
| Missing | 0.0 |

A counter-argument must include two parts:

1. an opposing view,
2. a rebuttal.

A concession is not automatically a counter-argument.

---

## Excellent Counter-argument, 1.0

Assign **Excellent** when the paragraph has:

- a real opposing view,
- a clear and logical rebuttal,
- relevant evidence or a strong example,
- explanation of why the rebuttal is stronger,
- a coherent closing sentence.

The counter-argument can be Excellent even if the language has errors, as long as the opposing view and rebuttal are clear and convincing.

---

## Good Counter-argument, 0.8

Assign **Good** when:

- there is a real opposing view,
- the rebuttal is clear,
- the response is reasonable,
- there is some evidence, example, or methodological response,
- the wording may be imprecise, but the logic is understandable.

A methodological rebuttal can receive Good if the student questions the quality, scope, or validity of the opposing evidence.

---

## Satisfactory Counter-argument, 0.6

Assign **Satisfactory** when:

- there is a recognizable opposing view,
- there is a rebuttal,
- the development is general, brief, or not fully convincing,
- the evidence is limited,
- the paragraph fulfills the basic function but lacks depth.

---

## Needs Improvement Counter-argument, 0.4

Assign **Needs Improvement** when:

- the opposing view is vague,
- the rebuttal is weak,
- the response is mostly opinion-based,
- the paragraph is too brief,
- the evidence is missing or unclear.

A real counter-argument can still be Needs Improvement if it is underdeveloped.

---

## Poor Counter-argument, 0.2

Assign **Poor** when:

- there is no meaningful opposing view,
- the rebuttal is not functional,
- the paragraph is incoherent,
- the student only makes a small concession,
- the section does not really respond to an opposing position.

Example:

> Technology makes life easier.

This is only a concession. It is not a counter-argument unless the student develops it as an opposing view and refutes it.

---

## Missing Counter-argument, 0.0

Assign **0.0** if there is no attempt at a counter-argument.

If there is a very weak attempt, use Poor instead of 0.0.

---

# 7. Conclusion

## Maximum Score: 0.5

| Level | Score |
|---|---:|
| Excellent | 0.5 |
| Good | 0.4 |
| Satisfactory | 0.3 |
| Needs Improvement | 0.2 |
| Poor | 0.1 |
| Missing or incomplete | 0.0 |

---

## Excellent Conclusion, 0.5

Assign **Excellent** when the conclusion:

- restates the thesis,
- synthesizes the main arguments,
- refers to the counter-argument or final position when relevant,
- closes the essay effectively,
- includes a final reflection, recommendation, or call to action.

The conclusion can be Excellent even with some language errors if it clearly fulfills its function.

---

## Good Conclusion, 0.4

Assign **Good** when:

- there is a real conclusion,
- the thesis is restated,
- the main ideas are summarized,
- there is a closing move,
- the final sentence may be awkward but still functions.

A conclusion can be Good even if one phrase is confusing, as long as the conclusion closes the essay and returns to the main argument.

---

## Satisfactory Conclusion, 0.3

Assign **Satisfactory** when:

- there is a real ending,
- the student restates some ideas,
- the conclusion is general, repetitive, or abstract,
- the synthesis is weak,
- the closing move is present but not strong.

A conclusion that proposes a solution but does not synthesize the body paragraphs well can be Satisfactory.

---

## Needs Improvement Conclusion, 0.2

Assign **Needs Improvement** when:

- the conclusion is vague,
- the thesis is not clearly restated,
- the closing idea is weak,
- the paragraph barely connects to the essay.

---

## Poor Conclusion, 0.1

Assign **Poor** when:

- the conclusion barely functions,
- it introduces unrelated ideas,
- it does not close the discussion,
- it is extremely weak but still present.

---

## Missing or Incomplete Conclusion, 0.0

Assign **0.0** when:

- there is no conclusion,
- the conclusion is cut off,
- the essay ends before the conclusion is developed,
- the final sentence is incomplete.

---

# 8. Grammar & Mechanics

## Maximum Score: 0.8

| Level | Score |
|---|---:|
| Excellent | 0.8 |
| Good | 0.64 |
| Satisfactory | 0.48 |
| Needs Improvement | 0.32 |
| Poor | 0.16 |

Grade Grammar & Mechanics according to readability and clarity, not perfection.

---

## Excellent Grammar & Mechanics, 0.8

Assign **Excellent** when:

- the essay is clear and readable,
- the prose is coherent,
- vocabulary is appropriate or ambitious for B1+,
- errors are local and do not seriously affect comprehension,
- the student communicates complex ideas successfully.

Excellent does not mean error-free. A student may have several local errors and still receive Excellent if the overall control is strong for B1+.

---

## Good Grammar & Mechanics, 0.64

Assign **Good** when:

- there are several errors,
- the message is generally clear,
- the reader does not struggle much,
- the vocabulary is mostly appropriate,
- errors do not seriously block comprehension.

This can apply even when the essay is incomplete, because missing sections should be penalized in content, not automatically in Grammar & Mechanics.

---

## Satisfactory Grammar & Mechanics, 0.48

Assign **Satisfactory** when:

- there are frequent errors,
- some sentences require effort,
- word choice is sometimes inaccurate,
- punctuation and sentence structure are inconsistent,
- the general message is still understandable.

Use Satisfactory when the reader needs some effort, but not constant effort.

---

## Needs Improvement Grammar & Mechanics, 0.32

Assign **Needs Improvement** when:

- errors are frequent,
- several ideas are difficult to interpret,
- sentence structure often affects clarity,
- word choice repeatedly interferes with meaning,
- punctuation problems make the text hard to follow,
- the reader must work frequently to reconstruct the ideas.

Use Needs Improvement when the whole essay requires repeated effort to understand.

---

## Poor Grammar & Mechanics, 0.16

Assign **Poor** when:

- errors are pervasive,
- meaning is often unclear,
- many sentences are incoherent,
- the essay is very difficult to follow.

---

# 9. Evidence and Warrant Rules

## 9.1 Evidence must be explained

Do not give a high score just because the student mentions a source.

A strong paragraph needs:

- claim,
- evidence,
- warrant.

The warrant explains why the evidence supports the claim.

---

## 9.2 Mentioning a study is not enough

A paragraph should not automatically receive Good or Excellent because it says:

- “studies show,”
- “according to research,”
- “according to UNESCO,”
- “a university study says.”

The student must explain how that evidence proves the argument.

---

## 9.3 General evidence usually limits the score

If the evidence is vague or general, the paragraph is usually:

- Satisfactory,
- Good at most,
- rarely Excellent.

---

## 9.4 Specific examples can strengthen a paragraph

Specific examples can help a paragraph reach Excellent when they are clearly connected to the thesis.

Examples of strong specificity:

- local communities,
- named programs,
- specific laws,
- concrete studies,
- specific cases,
- relevant statistics,
- clear contextual examples.

---

## 9.5 Hypothetical examples are weaker

Hypothetical examples can illustrate an idea, but they do not always prove the claim.

If a paragraph relies mostly on hypothetical or speculative reasoning, it is often Satisfactory, even if the idea is interesting.

---

# 10. Common Grading Decisions

## 10.1 When to be generous

Be generous when:

- the essay is clear for B1+,
- the argument is understandable,
- the section fulfills its function,
- the student includes a relevant example,
- grammar errors do not block comprehension,
- the essay shows a complete argumentative structure,
- the counter-argument and rebuttal are recognizable.

---

## 10.2 When to be strict

Be strict when:

- body paragraphs are mostly opinion-based,
- evidence is not explained,
- ideas are repeated instead of developed,
- the thesis is confusing,
- the roadmap is unclear,
- the counter-argument is only a concession,
- the conclusion is missing or incomplete,
- unrelated ideas appear without being integrated,
- language problems repeatedly affect clarity.

---

## 10.3 Do not confuse ambition with quality

An essay can have an ambitious topic, sophisticated vocabulary, or complex reasoning and still receive Satisfactory in the body paragraphs if the evidence and warrant are not convincing.

Interesting ideas are not enough. The paragraph must prove the claim.

---

## 10.4 Do not confuse lexical awkwardness with poor grammar

Expressions may sound unnatural, but if the essay is readable and coherent, Grammar & Mechanics can still be Good or Excellent.

Examples of lexical awkwardness:

- “create a trust,”
- “give trust,”
- “at the time to buy,”
- “secure methods.”

These are problems, but they do not automatically lower Grammar & Mechanics if comprehension remains strong.

---

# 11. Score Anchors

## 5.0 Essay

A 5.0 essay has:

- excellent introduction,
- excellent body paragraphs,
- excellent counter-argument,
- excellent conclusion,
- excellent grammar and mechanics.

It has a strong thesis, well-developed arguments, relevant evidence, clear warrants, a real counter-argument with a strong rebuttal, and polished language for B1+.

---

## Around 4.5

A 4.5 essay is very strong for B1+.

It may have grammar or word-choice issues, but:

- the structure is complete,
- the position is clear,
- the arguments are relevant,
- the counter-argument works,
- the conclusion is effective,
- the message is understandable.

---

## Around 4.0

A 4.0 essay is strong but uneven.

It may have:

- excellent introduction or conclusion,
- strong language control,
- good counter-argument,
- but body paragraphs that are only Satisfactory because the evidence or reasoning is not fully convincing.

---

## Around 3.5

A 3.5 essay is complete and understandable, but development is limited.

It often has:

- clear position,
- complete structure,
- general evidence,
- repetitive ideas,
- shallow warrants,
- functional but not strong counter-argument.

---

## Around 3.0

A 3.0 essay has recognizable structure and ideas, but serious weaknesses.

It may have:

- confusing introduction,
- one good body paragraph,
- one weak body paragraph,
- good or satisfactory counter-argument,
- conclusion that closes the essay,
- grammar that often affects clarity.

---

## Around 2.2

A 2.2 essay has a general position but weak development.

It often has:

- imprecise thesis,
- body paragraphs that are mostly opinion-based,
- unsupported arguments,
- poor counter-argument,
- basic conclusion,
- grammar that requires effort but remains understandable.

---

## Around 1.7

A 1.7 essay is incomplete.

It may have:

- missing Body 2,
- incomplete conclusion,
- weak introduction,
- one partially developed body paragraph,
- poor counter-argument,
- grammar that is acceptable in the parts that exist.

---

# 12. Step-by-Step Grading Procedure

## Step 1. Identify the thesis

Before grading, identify:

- the student’s main position,
- argument 1,
- argument 2,
- the opposing view.

If these cannot be identified, lower Introduction and probably the body paragraph scores.

---

## Step 2. Grade content before grammar

First evaluate:

1. Introduction
2. Body 1
3. Body 2
4. Counter-argument
5. Conclusion

Then evaluate:

6. Grammar & Mechanics

---

## Step 3. Check each body paragraph

For each body paragraph, ask:

- Is there a clear claim?
- Is there evidence or an example?
- Is the evidence connected to the claim?
- Is there a warrant?
- Does the paragraph support the thesis?
- Does the paragraph stay focused?

---

## Step 4. Check the counter-argument

Ask:

- Is there a real opposing view?
- Is there a rebuttal?
- Is the rebuttal logical?
- Is there evidence or reasoning?
- Does the rebuttal answer the opposing view?

If the answer is mostly no, assign Poor or Needs Improvement.

---

## Step 5. Check the conclusion

Ask:

- Does it restate the thesis?
- Does it synthesize the main arguments?
- Does it close the essay?
- Is it complete?

If the conclusion is missing or cut off, assign 0.0.

---

## Step 6. Grade Grammar & Mechanics by readability

Ask:

- Is the text easy to understand?
- Does the reader need some effort or constant effort?
- Do errors block comprehension?
- Is vocabulary appropriate for the topic?
- Are the sentences coherent?

Do not grade Grammar & Mechanics by counting errors mechanically.

---

---

# 14. Feedback Style

Feedback should be:

- concise,
- student-friendly,
- specific,
- balanced,
- written in English,
- focused on the most important improvement areas.

Do not write very long feedback. Mention what the student did well and one or two key areas to improve.

---

## Suggested Feedback Structure

1. Start with a strength.
2. Mention the main weakness.
3. Give a concrete recommendation.
4. End with encouragement.

Example:

> Your essay presents a clear position and follows the basic structure of an argumentative essay. The strongest part is that you include a relevant counter-argument and try to respond to it. However, your body paragraphs need stronger evidence and clearer explanations of how the examples support your claims. Try to develop each paragraph with a clear topic sentence, specific evidence, and a final sentence that connects the idea back to your thesis. Overall, you have a good foundation, but the argument needs more development and precision.

---
