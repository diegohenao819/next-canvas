According to the World Health Organization (2022) health is defined as “a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being” not merely an absence of disease or infirmity. This definition emphasizes that mental health is an inseparable part of overall health. Moreover, the United Nations’ Universal Declaration of Human Rights (Article 25) recognizes the right of every person to a standard of living adequate for health and well-being, including medical care. These declarations demonstrate that mental health is not optional; it is a core part of what it means to be healthy and to live with dignity. Therefore, universal access to psychological care is a matter of justice and equality, not a privilege. When communities recognize this right, they commit to value emotional and psychological well-being as highly as physical health, thereby creating conditions where all individuals can thrive. Consequently, understanding mental health as a fundamental right compels governments to ensure psychological care for all, as true equality in health includes both body and mind.

Furthermore, providing free mental health services can significantly reduce long-term social and economic costs for both individuals and society. According to the World Health Organization (2019) depression and anxiety disorders cost the global economy one trillion US dollars annually in lost productivity. Similarly, the National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI, 2023) reports that untreated mental health conditions contribute to higher unemployment rates and increased healthcare expenses. These findings demonstrate that neglecting mental health services can create a financial burden that goes beyond the individual level. These results demonstrate that financial accessibility and system capacity are not opposing goals, but mutually reinforcing aspects. This cycle of investment and participation gradually strengthens mental health infrastructure, transforming what was once a limitation into a source of national resilience and social progress. Therefore, rather than weakening the mental health systems, making services free can drive governments to build sustainable infrastructures and ensure equitable access for all. Ultimately, financial accessibility empowers communities to create stronger, more inclusive and more resilient mental health systems.

In conclusion, making mental health services universally free is not merely a policy choice, but a moral and practical necessity that safeguards both individual dignity and social stability. Indeed, psychological care is a fundamental human right and its recognition as such should ensure free access to mental health services. Sequentially, public investment in these systems provides social stability and individual productivity, reducing long-term costs. Although some argue that this access would not be practical due to infrastructure issues, investment in such services actually strengthens the overall existing systems and provides tools for new and better networks. Therefore, recognizing psychological care as a public priority requires action, not just awareness. Governments and communities must collaborate to ensure mental health services for everyone, treating mental health with the same urgency as physical health to promote equality and collective well-being.
